Teaching Social Studies in the Age of AI

Preserving inquiry, agency, and democratic learning when machines can produce the answer.

Micah Miner C.O.R.E. / H.E.A.R.T. Pedagogical friction
National Louis University / virtual session1
Open by naming the tension in plain language: social studies has always taught students to interpret sources, hold competing claims, and develop civic judgment. Generative AI can help that work, but it can also replace the work students needed to do to become citizens capable of judgment.
Start with the classroom

Two students turn in the same polished historical argument.

Student A used AI to enter the work.

They tested claims, compared sources, revised language, and defended decisions.

Student B used AI to exit the work.

They accepted a fluent answer without doing the inquiry the task was built to develop.

Same artifact. Different learning.2
This frames the whole session: the product alone cannot tell us whether learning happened. In social studies, the process matters because the task is often designed to build sourcing, contextualization, deliberation, empathy, evidence use, and civic agency.
Core claim

AI supports learning when it helps students enter, sustain, or deepen the work of thinking.

It bypasses learning when it replaces the thinking the task was designed to develop.

From tool question to learning question3
Name the productive and risky sides together. This is not a ban-AI talk or an AI hype talk. It is a design talk: what kind of human work does this assignment require, and what role should AI play around that work?

Ground this in your background as a social studies educator and technology leader. Social studies develops habits of inquiry and participation. The AI question is not only, "Can students get an answer?" It is, "Are students still practicing the habits that democratic life requires?"
The debate is too small

We keep asking whether AI is cheating.

The deeper question is what happens when AI can produce the work students used to do in order to learn.

Integrity Assessment Cognition Agency Democracy
Academic integrity is real. It is not the whole story.5
This connects to the doctoral colloquium argument while making it social-studies-specific. Cheating is an institutional concern. Learning is the educational concern. Civic agency is the social studies concern.
The paradox

Frictionless learning can become frictionless non-learning.

AI makes the artifact easier.

Summaries, arguments, counterclaims, examples, and lesson products appear quickly.

→

But learning needs process.

Confusion, dialogue, revision, source conflict, and ownership build durable understanding.

Artifact vs. process6
Use the Drive article's argument: generative AI promises efficiency and personalization, but can undermine deliberate cognitive and social struggle. The goal is not to make learning artificially hard. The goal is to preserve the parts of difficulty that produce understanding.
Theory in plain language

Four older ideas still help us see the AI problem.

Dewey

Students learn through meaningful experience, not passive receipt.

Vygotsky

Thinking grows through social mediation and guided stretch.

Piaget

Disequilibrium changes schemas; easy clarity can block growth.

Freire

Education should create agency, not compliance with delivered answers.

Constructivism becomes urgent again.7
Keep this short in the virtual talk. The audience does not need a literature lecture; they need a usable lens. These theories converge around active meaning-making, social interaction, struggle, and agency.
Media ecology

AI is not just another resource students consult.

It is a medium that can reorganize how students encounter knowledge, authority, evidence, and voice.

SearchStudents choose among sources.
FeedsPlatforms curate what becomes visible.
Generative AISystems curate and create the symbolic material itself.
The medium shapes the work.8
Connect McLuhan and Ong without crowding the slide. In social studies, the way information arrives matters. A student does different cognitive work when wrestling with primary sources than when receiving a plausible synthetic summary.

Design principle

Pedagogical friction is useful difficulty by design.

It protects the thinking, explaining, revising, and meaning-making that learning requires.

Not busywork Not exclusion Not anti-AI A learning safeguard
Reduce barriers. Protect thinking.10
Define pedagogical friction clearly. Distinguish productive friction from needless difficulty. This is an accessibility point: remove barriers that prevent participation while preserving the cognitive work that develops understanding.

Use decision tool

Ask four questions before using AI in a social studies task.

What thinking is this task meant to develop?
What barrier could AI responsibly reduce?
What cognitive or civic work must stay human?
How will students explain, verify, and own the result?
A practical pause point12
This is the central tool for the virtual session. Participants can use it immediately for DBQs, historical writing, civic simulations, debate prep, source analysis, and media literacy lessons.
When AI helps

Use AI around the inquiry, not instead of the inquiry.

A

Access

Vocabulary support, reading-level bridges, translation drafts.

B

Practice

Low-stakes questioning, claim testing, counterexample generation.

C

Feedback

Ask for missing evidence, unclear reasoning, or alternative interpretations.

D

Reflection

Compare AI output with student judgment and source evidence.

Scaffold without surrendering the task.13
Make this balanced. AI can support inclusion and access when it lowers barriers to participation. But the teacher must define what cannot be outsourced: sourcing, evidence judgment, perspective-taking, and civic reasoning.
Example redesign

Instead of “write the argument,” redesign the process.

Before AIStudents annotate two primary sources and write a claim from evidence.
With AIStudents ask AI for a rival interpretation, then test it against the sources.
After AIStudents explain which interpretation is stronger, what AI missed, and what they changed.
The artifact improves because the thinking deepens.14
This is a concrete social studies example. The point is not to hide AI from the task but to situate it as a challenge, critic, or contrast case. The student still has to read, judge, and explain.
Participant activity

Redesign one task in 8 minutes.

Choose a task

DBQ, current event analysis, historical essay, civic debate, media literacy check, or project reflection.

Add one pause point

Students must verify, compare, explain, revise, or defend before the artifact is complete.

Make the tool useful before making it scalable.15
For a virtual session, use chat or breakout rooms. Ask participants to post: task, AI role, protected thinking, evidence of ownership. Keep the share-out focused on design decisions, not tool demonstrations.
From classroom to system

Policies should protect learning, not only police tools.

1

Learning

Name the human work students must practice.

2

Privacy

Limit data exposure and avoid sensitive student inputs.

3

Equity

Ensure AI support does not become lower expectations.

4

Transparency

Require disclosure, reflection, and teacher-visible process.

Governance is instructional design at scale.16
Connect district leadership to classroom practice. Policies that focus only on detection miss the pedagogical issue. The better policy question is: how do we protect privacy, equity, and the conditions for human learning?

Closing claim

In social studies, the goal is not faster answers.

The goal is students who can investigate, deliberate, judge evidence, and participate responsibly in public life.

AI should strengthen that work, not quietly remove it.

Discussion18
End with democratic purpose. Invite discussion around one question: where can AI reduce access barriers without removing the civic and cognitive work social studies is designed to cultivate?
